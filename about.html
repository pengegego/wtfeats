About WTFeats

Chinese food does not need defending. It needs explaining.

WTFeats is a field guide to Chinese foods that look terrifying, taste incredible, and deserve a proper explanation — the history, the chemistry, and the craft. No fear-mongering. No exoticism. Just facts.

The problem

Chinese food has been called disgusting on television, ranked on "worst foods" lists, and reduced to shock content — usually by people who never understood what they were eating. A century egg becomes "the egg buried for a hundred years in horse urine." Stinky tofu becomes a dare. Silkworm pupae become a gross-out segment between commercial breaks.

The format is always the same: hold the food at arm's length, make a face, move on. Nobody stays long enough to ask why it looks that way, how it is made, or why hundreds of millions of people love it.

The corrective

Every food on this site gets the explanation it deserves. A century egg is not a century old — it is roughly thirty days of alkaline chemistry, sitting on top of 1,300 years of documented history. Stinky tofu's smell is a controlled fermentation, chemically related to the washed-rind cheeses Europe is proud of. The silkworm pupa is not a curiosity — it is one of the most nutrient-dense foods in the animal kingdom, with a terroir of its own.

When you understand the craft, the fear tends to evaporate. What remains is appetite.

Our editorial principles
  • No fear-mongering. We do not use "disgusting" as a hook. The food is the story, not the reaction to it.
  • No exoticism. These are everyday foods for hundreds of millions of people, not props.
  • Show the science. Gelation, fermentation, Maillard browning — the chemistry is explained in plain English, never waved away.
  • Check the numbers. Nutritional and historical claims are verified against published food-science literature and culinary history sources before they appear on a page.
  • Admit what we don't know. Regional recipes vary; where traditions differ, we say so.

How each food is covered

Every food gets a four-part treatment, and you can enter at any level of curiosity:

  • Overview — the friendly version: what it is, what it tastes like, and which rumors are wrong.
  • Visual timeline — the transformation, stage by stage, from raw ingredient to finished dish.
  • Science — the deep dive: chemistry, microbiology, and nutrition, written to be read, not endured.
  • Chapters — the full table of contents, including how people actually eat it.

Who makes this

WTFeats is an independent editorial project. It is not funded by any restaurant, brand, or tourism board, and no food appears here because someone paid for placement. Foods are chosen by one criterion only: they are misunderstood, and the true story is better than the rumor.

The archive is growing. Century egg, stinky tofu, and silkworm pupa are documented; more specimens are being prepared.

Nothing here is a dare. Everything here is dinner.

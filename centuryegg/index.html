The Process

The Four Stages of Transformation

Each stage paired with its underlying chemistry — from raw egg to culinary masterpiece.

Stage I

Liquefaction & Penetration

Days 1–7

Alkaline compounds (NaOH) breach the shell\'s micro-pores. The egg white completely liquefies. Extreme high pH attacks mucin proteins, causing complete hydrolysis — tightly wound protein chains uncoil and separate, dismantling the egg\'s structural foundation. Critical: Do not move or shake the curing vessel.

Stage II

Coagulation & Gelation

Days 7–15

The egg white re-solidifies into a highly elastic, semi-transparent jelly. The yolk\'s outer layer coagulates and turns dark green through saponification. Unspooled proteins chemically denature and reconnect, forging a massive water-locking 3D polymer network. Metallic ions plug shell pores to slow further alkaline penetration. A pungent ammonia scent peaks.

Stage III

Coloration & The Maillard Magic

Days 16–30

Maillard reactions transform the gel into rich deep amber. The yolk's outer layer turns ink-green from iron sulfide. The center remains a sticky, molten paste — the coveted Tangxin.

Mg²⁺ + Amino Acids → Magnesium Hydroxide Hydrates — the signature pine-flower crystals precipitate within the gel matrix.

Stage IV

Maturation & Flavor Fixing

Days 31–45

Structural stability peaks — the egg white achieves a jade-like finish. The Tangxin size is permanently fixed. CO₂ from the air slowly permeates the shell, neutralizing residual alkalinity and eliminating the sharp lime scent. A mellow umami aroma emerges. Risk: if left too long, the egg reverts to liquid with a harsh odor.

History

From Accidental Discovery
to Culinary Icon

The century egg's origin story is as surprising as its appearance.

According to popular legend, the century egg was discovered during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644) in Hunan province. A farmer found duck eggs in a pit of slaked lime that had been forgotten months earlier. Instead of rotten, the eggs had transformed into something translucent and fragrant.

While the Ming Dynasty tale is the most well-known, alkaline preservation of eggs dates back even further in Chinese history. By the Qing Dynasty, century eggs had become a staple of Chinese appetizer culture, spreading across the country and eventually the world.

Today, century eggs are enjoyed across East and Southeast Asia, with modern production methods replacing traditional clay coatings with brine and vacuum packaging — though the chemistry remains unchanged.

How to Eat

Three Classic Ways
to Enjoy Century Egg

Simple preparations that let the egg speak for itself.

Classic

Century Egg Tofu

The simplest and most popular preparation. Silken tofu sliced and laid flat, topped with wedges of century egg, then drizzled with soy sauce, black vinegar, and sesame oil. Finished with scallions and cilantro. Served cold — ready in three minutes.

Comfort

Century Egg Congee

A Cantonese breakfast classic. Rice simmered until silky, then mixed with chopped century egg and marinated lean pork. Finished with white pepper and scallions. The egg's richness melts into the congee, creating a deeply savory, comforting bowl.

Simple

Ginger & Soy

The purest way to taste century egg. Wedges arranged on a plate, topped with a drizzle of ginger-infused black vinegar and chili oil. No other ingredients — just the egg and the sharp, aromatic contrast of fresh ginger.
